<h2>Gamma-Ray Bursts (GRBs)</h2>
<p>Gamma-Ray Bursts (<strong>GRBs</strong>) are the most powerful explosions observed in the universe, releasing vast amounts of energy in just seconds. These high-energy events are divided into two types based on their duration:</p>

<ul>
    <li><strong>Short GRBs (SGRBs)</strong> – Lasting <strong>less than 2 seconds</strong>, typically associated with the mergers of <strong>neutron stars</strong> or a <strong>neutron star–black hole binary</strong>.</li>
    <li><strong>Long GRBs (LGRBs)</strong> – Lasting <strong>more than 2 seconds</strong>, linked to the <strong>collapse of massive stars</strong> in supernova or hypernova events.</li>
</ul>


<h2>Orphan Afterglows: The Silent Remnants of Gamma-Ray Bursts</h2>
<p>Not all Gamma-Ray Bursts (GRBs) are detected as bright flashes of gamma rays. Some GRB jets are <strong>misaligned</strong> from our line of sight, making the initial burst invisible while their <strong>afterglow</strong>—emission in X-ray, optical, and radio—slowly becomes detectable as the jet expands. These are known as <strong>orphan afterglows</strong>.</p>

<p>Unlike typical GRBs, where the gamma-ray flash triggers observations, orphan afterglows are discovered <strong>serendipitously</strong> in transient surveys like <strong>ZTF, LSST, and radio observatories</strong>. Their detection helps <strong>constrain GRB jet structures, event rates, and the physics of relativistic shocks</strong>. Studying orphan afterglows provides crucial insights into <strong>the true population of GRBs</strong>, many of which go unnoticed due to jet orientation.</p>
