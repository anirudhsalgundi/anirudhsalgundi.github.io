Optical Transients

1. What are Optical Transients?

Astrophysical transients are short-lived celestial events that appear and evolve rapidly across the sky, often due to energetic phenomena like stellar explosions, compact object mergers, or accretion instabilities. These include supernovae, gamma-ray bursts, kilonovae, and tidal disruption events. Studying transients helps us probe the extreme physics of the universe and explore connections between light, gravitational waves, and high-energy particles in the era of multi-messenger astronomy.

2. Discoveries and Follow-up

Discovery and follow-up of astrophysical transients rely on a network of wide-field and targeted telescopes. The Zwicky Transient Facility (ZTF) scans large areas of the northern sky every night, rapidly identifying new transients such as supernovae, gamma-ray burst afterglows, and kilonova candidates. The Dark Energy Camera (DECam) on the Blanco 4m telescope offers deep imaging capabilities crucial for follow-up of faint or distant events, especially those triggered by gravitational wave detections. The GROWTH-India Telescope (GIT), a 70-cm robotic facility, complements this effort by enabling high-cadence monitoring from India, improving temporal coverage and enabling rapid photometric characterization. Together, these facilities form a powerful global network for real-time transient science and multi-messenger astronomy.
